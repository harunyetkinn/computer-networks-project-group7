§ 01

What Is the Internet?

🔩

Nuts & Bolts View

A packet-switching infrastructure that connects billions of devices. It consists of routers, switches, and communication links across different physical media.

Hardware-Focused
🛎️

Services View

A platform that provides the infrastructure for applications such as the web, video streaming, email, online games, and e-commerce. It offers a programming interface (API) for distributed applications.

Software-Focused
🌐

A Network of Networks

The Internet is made up of interconnected Internet Service Providers (ISPs). Home, enterprise, mobile, and data center networks connect to local ISPs, which in turn connect to global ISPs.

Hierarchy
📡

Internet of Things (IoT)

Countless devices are connected to the Internet — smartphones, cars, refrigerators, smart watches, pacemakers, and more. As of 2023, there are approximately 15 billion connected devices.

IoT
📌 Key Components: End Systems (Hosts) – devices running at the edge of the network  |  Packet Switches – routers and switches that forward data blocks  |  Communication Links – fiber, copper, radio, satellite  |  Bandwidth – transmission rate measured in bps

Network Edge: Access Networks

🏠

DSL (Digital Subscriber Line)

Uses the existing telephone line. Voice and data are transmitted on different frequencies. Download: 24–52 Mbps, Upload: 3.5–16 Mbps.

Wired
📺

Cable Access (HFC)

Hybrid fiber-coaxial network. Data and TV share the cable on different frequencies. Download: 40 Mbps – 1.2 Gbps.

Shared
📶

Wireless Access

WiFi (802.11): ~100m range, 11–450 Mbps. 4G/5G: Tens of kilometers, tens to hundreds of Mbps. Connection to a router via a base station.

Wireless
🏢

Enterprise Network

Universities and companies use a mix of Ethernet (100 Mbps–10 Gbps) and WiFi. Switches and routers connect the network to an ISP.

Hybrid

Guided: Twisted pair (Cat5/6), coaxial cable, fiber optic. Unguided: Radio waves (WiFi, 4G/5G, Bluetooth, satellite).

Carries pulses of light through glass fiber. Speed: 10–100 Gbps, low error rate, not affected by electromagnetic interference.

§ 04

Network Core: Packet vs. Circuit Switching

📦 Packet Switching
  • Messages are divided into small packets
  • Store-and-forward: packet is not forwarded until fully received
  • Resources are shared, flexible usage
  • Ideal for bursty data traffic
  • Queuing delays and packet loss can occur
  • Used by the Internet
🔗 Circuit Switching
  • End-to-end resources are reserved in advance
  • Guaranteed bandwidth and fixed delay
  • An idle circuit wastes resources
  • Suitable for continuous communication (voice, PSTN)
  • Multiplexing via FDM or TDM
  • Used by traditional telephone networks

💡 Numerical Example: A 1 Gbps link where each user needs 100 Mbps when active and is active only 10% of the time. Circuit switching supports only 10 users, while with packet switching and 35 users, the probability of more than 10 being active at once is < 0.0004.

🗺️ Routing

A global action: determines the source-to-destination path for packets. Routing algorithms consider the entire network to compute the best path.

↔️ Forwarding

A local action: moves an incoming packet from the router's input port to the correct output port using a local forwarding table.

🏗️ ISP Hierarchy

Access ISP → Regional ISP → Tier-1 ISP (AT&T, NTT, etc.) → IXP (Internet Exchange Point). Companies like Google and Facebook manage their own networks.

§ 05

Performance: Delay, Loss, and Throughput

dnodal = dproc + dqueue + dtrans + dprop

⚙️ Processing Delay (dproc)

The time a router needs to check for bit errors and determine the output link. Typically in the range of microseconds.

< μsec

🚦 Queuing Delay (dqueue)

The time a packet waits to be transmitted on the output link. Depends on traffic intensity (La/R). Delay grows as La/R approaches 1.

Variable

📡 Transmission Delay (dtrans)

Time to push an L-bit packet onto the link at rate R bps: dtrans = L/R. Example: 10 Kbit packet on a 100 Mbps link → 0.1 ms.

L/R

🌍 Propagation Delay (dprop)

Time for a bit to travel through the physical medium: dprop = d/s. Speed of light ~2×10⁸ m/s. 1000 km of fiber → ~5 ms.

d/s

📉 Packet Loss

When router buffers are full, arriving packets are dropped. A lost packet may be retransmitted by the previous node, the source, or not at all. TCP performs retransmission when loss is detected.

📊 Throughput

Bits per unit of time from sender to receiver. End-to-end throughput is determined by the slowest link on the path — the bottleneck link: min(Rs, Rc).

§ 06

Network Security

The Internet was originally designed without security in mind — it assumed "mutually trusting users." Today, security measures are required at every layer.

🕵️ Packet Sniffing

A network interface in promiscuous mode can capture all packets on a shared medium (Ethernet, WiFi), including passwords and other sensitive data. Tools like Wireshark are commonly used for this.

🎭 IP Spoofing

Injecting packets with a fake source IP address. The attacker pretends to be someone else.

💥 DoS / DDoS Attack

Flooding a target server with fake traffic to prevent legitimate users from accessing it. Often carried out using botnet networks.

🛡️

Defense Methods

Authentication · Encryption (confidentiality) · Digital signatures (integrity) · Firewall · VPN access control.

Multi-Layer Defense
§ 07

Protocol Layers

A layered architecture that breaks a complex network system into manageable parts. Each layer uses the services of the layer below it and provides services to the layer above.

5 · Application HTTP · SMTP · DNS · FTP
4 · Transport TCP · UDP
3 · Network IP · Routing Protocols
2 · Data Link Ethernet · 802.11 · PPP
1 · Physical Bits on the wire

Encapsulation

M → Message (Application)
Ht + M → Segment (Transport)
Hn + Ht + M → Datagram (Network)
Hl + Hn + Ht + M → Frame (Data Link)
0 1 0 1 1 … → Bits (Physical)

Each layer adds its own header to the data from the layer above — like nested Russian dolls.

§ 08

History of the Internet

1961–1972

The Birth of Packet Switching

Kleinrock developed queuing theory. ARPANET was designed in 1967, and the first node went live in 1969.

1972–1980

Internetworking & New Networks

Cerf and Kahn designed the TCP/IP architecture to connect networks together. Ethernet was developed at Xerox PARC.

1980–1990

TCP/IP and DNS

TCP/IP was standardized in 1983, and DNS was defined. New national networks like NSFnet and BITnet were established. 100,000+ hosts.

1990–2000

The Web and the Commercial Era

Berners-Lee developed HTML & HTTP. Mosaic launched in 1994, followed by Netscape. The commercialization of the Web. 50M+ hosts.

2005–Present

Mobile, Cloud, SDN

4G/5G, software-defined networking (SDN), cloud computing (AWS, Azure), and the dominance of smartphones. ~15 billion connected devices.
